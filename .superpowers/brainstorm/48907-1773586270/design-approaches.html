A: The Workshop

Warm paper palette, quiet confidence. Cream backgrounds (#FAF7F2), earthy type colors, a single thin accent line. Monospace for labels/numbering, serif for the headline. Products listed with simple numbering like a craftsman's catalog. Minimal, editorial, feels like opening a well-made notebook.

Pros

  • Maximum distance from AI-startup aesthetic
  • Warm and inviting immediately
  • Typography does all the work

Cons

  • Might feel too quiet for a tech company
  • Serif headline is a departure from the monospace brief

B: The Builder's Log (Recommended)

Monospace-forward, warm canvas, confident scale. Large monospace headlines on a warm linen background (#F5F0E8). Products in clean bordered cards with monospace labels and arrows. The accent color (#C4956A) only appears on the "not demos" punchline and product labels. Feels like a maker's project board — organized, intentional, built by hand.

Pros

  • Monospace headlines match the brief exactly
  • Strong visual identity — instantly distinctive
  • Warm without being soft — confident and direct
  • Natural asymmetry in product sections

Cons

  • Large monospace can feel heavy if not balanced well
  • Needs good font choice (not all monospace fonts are warm)

C: The Broadsheet

Editorial grid, newspaper-inspired structure. Off-white (#FFFBF5) with horizontal rules, monospace labels as section headers, sans-serif body. Products in an asymmetric editorial grid — one featured, others listed. Location included like a masthead. Feels like a well-designed independent publication.

Pros

  • Strong editorial identity
  • Natural hierarchy (featured vs listed)
  • Horizontal rules add structure without cards

Cons

  • Could feel too "publication" and not enough "product company"
  • Asymmetric grid needs careful balancing on mobile
